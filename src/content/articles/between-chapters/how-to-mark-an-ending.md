## Why unmarked endings linger

An ending that simply fades out—a job you drift away from, a friendship that quietly stops—can be harder to move past than one that is acknowledged clearly. Marking it gives your mind a defined point to file it under "finished."

## Simple ways to mark an ending

- **Write a short, honest letter** you don't have to send, naming what the chapter gave you and what it cost.
- **Return or retire one physical object** connected to that chapter—clearing a drawer, donating an item, deleting a folder.
- **Revisit one place** connected to the chapter one last time, deliberately, rather than by accident.
- **Tell one person** the chapter is over, out loud, even if they already know.
- **Choose a specific date** to call it closed, rather than letting the ending stay vague indefinitely.

## What this isn't

Marking an ending is not the same as processing grief or trauma, and it doesn't replace support from people close to you or a professional if the loss is significant. It is a small, concrete practice for chapters that are ready to close but haven't yet been acknowledged.

## After the marking

Give yourself a specific next small step—even something as modest as planning one afternoon of the next chapter—so the ending has somewhere to lead rather than just stopping.
