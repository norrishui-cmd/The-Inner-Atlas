## Disorientation is not a warning sign

Feeling unmoored in the first weeks or months after a move is common, not a sign that the decision was wrong. Familiarity with a place takes longer to build than a change of address.

## What tends to help early on

Walking the same few routes repeatedly, rather than trying to "see everything," speeds up the sense of orientation. Finding one recurring anchor—a café, a park, a class—gives the week a shape faster than trying to build an entire social life at once.

## What tends to slow it down

Comparing the new city to the old one in every small moment keeps you emotionally in the place you left. It can help to notice the comparison, name it, and ask what this specific place actually offers on its own terms.

## Give it a real timeline

Feeling genuinely at home often takes months, not weeks. If you can, avoid making major additional decisions—another move, a big financial commitment—until you've given the new place enough time to be judged fairly.

## If the loneliness feels heavier than expected

Occasional loneliness after a move is ordinary. If it becomes persistent, isolating, or affects your daily functioning, that is worth discussing with a professional rather than waiting it out alone.
