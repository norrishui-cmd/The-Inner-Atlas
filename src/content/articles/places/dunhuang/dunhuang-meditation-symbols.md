## Begin with the image, not a promise

Dunhuang meditation symbols are visual entrances into attention. They are not universal energy codes, medical treatments, or guarantees of spiritual progress. A respectful practice begins by learning where an image came from, then deciding what it helps you notice today.

## Four symbols for a quiet practice

The **lotus** can become a prompt for returning to a center. The **cave** can mark a protected interval away from unnecessary input. **Feitian** figures invite attention to movement, music, and lightness. The **Three Hares** motif opens a question about interdependence: how can three bodies share one rhythm?

Read the documented stories of the [Dunhuang lotus symbol](/places/dunhuang/dunhuang-lotus-symbol/), [Three Hares](/places/dunhuang/three-hares-meaning/), and [Feitian](/places/dunhuang/feitian-spiritual-meaning/) before choosing one as a personal visual anchor.

## A five-minute Dunhuang-inspired meditation

Sit comfortably and choose one image. For one minute, describe only what you can see. For two minutes, notice your breath without forcing it. For the final two minutes, write one sentence beginning with “Today I return to…”. The object or image holds your attention; it does not do the practice for you.

## FAQ

### Are Dunhuang symbols Buddhist symbols?

Many are part of Buddhist art and devotional environments, while their meanings vary by period, cave, patron, and visual context. Avoid treating one modern interpretation as the only historical meaning.

### Can a Dunhuang symbol heal me?

No claim of healing is made here. A symbol can support reflection, remembrance, or a daily ritual, while health concerns belong with qualified professionals.
