## Sand is not only atmosphere

Images of Dunhuang often make windblown sand look cinematic. In reality, strong dust or sand events can sharply reduce visibility, disrupt transport, irritate eyes and airways, and make outdoor desert travel unsafe.

The China Meteorological Administration describes severe “black wind” events in Gansu’s Hexi Corridor and other inland desert regions as hazardous weather involving strong wind, dust, and extremely low visibility.

## Before going into exposed terrain

- Check a current official forecast and local warnings.
- Follow closures and instructions from site staff or guides.
- Avoid independent travel into remote desert areas during unstable conditions.
- Carry eye protection, a well-fitting mask, water, and charged communication equipment.
- If visibility deteriorates, seek established shelter and avoid driving unless authorities advise otherwise.

## Do not turn risk into ritual

Desert exposure is not a test of spiritual commitment. The responsible response to dangerous conditions is to change the plan. Quiet travel begins with attention to the real place.
