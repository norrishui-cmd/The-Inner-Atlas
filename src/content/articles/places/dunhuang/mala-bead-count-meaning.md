## A number with more than one explanation

A traditional mala is most commonly strung with 108 beads. Multiple explanations for this number circulate across Buddhist and Hindu sources, and no single account is treated as definitive.

## The explanations most commonly given

One frequently cited account, tied to the Mokugenji Sutra, describes reciting a name or mantra 108 times using a string of beads to eliminate 108 earthly afflictions. Another, more analytical explanation used in some Buddhist teaching divides human sensory experience into 108 categories: six senses, across three time periods, in two states, with three types of feeling. Hindu and Vedic traditions separately associate 108 with cosmic and astronomical patterns.

## The guru bead

Most full malas include a 109th bead—called the guru, sumeru, or bindu bead—that sits outside the count. Counting begins next to it, and in some traditions a practitioner reverses direction at the guru bead rather than crossing over it, marking the completion of one full round.

## Shorter malas and wrist versions

Not every mala-style bracelet has 108 beads. Fractional counts—27, 21, or 18 beads—are also traditional, and are more practical for everyday wrist wear than a full 108-bead strand, which is typically longer and used in seated practice.

## Choosing with this in mind

If a bead count matters to you, it's worth knowing which tradition and explanation a piece draws from, rather than treating "108" as an interchangeable design detail.
