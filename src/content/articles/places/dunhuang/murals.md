## Why the Dunhuang murals matter

UNESCO records approximately 45,000 square metres of murals across the preserved Mogao cave complex. Created and renewed across many centuries, they cannot be reduced to one period or one visual style.

The paintings are central to Buddhist cave sanctuaries, but their historical value reaches further. They preserve images of patrons, architecture, clothing, music, work, landscape, travel, and cultural exchange.

## Subjects inside the caves

Visitors and digital viewers may encounter Buddha assemblies, bodhisattvas, flying celestial figures, narrative paintings, donor portraits, decorative ceilings, and scenes connected with sutras and devotional practice.

Some caves also contain material evidence of ordinary and political worlds. UNESCO highlights depictions that include trade missions, workers, warriors, roads, caravans, rivers, and cities.

## Murals are not a single symbolic dictionary

Meanings depend on cave, date, patronage, religious context, and composition. A lotus, flying figure, or halo should not automatically be assigned a universal modern “energy.” Dunhuang Within separates documented context from our contemporary reflection.
