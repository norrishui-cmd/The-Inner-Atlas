## What is a Dunhuang caisson ceiling?

Often called a *zaojing* in Chinese, the painted ceiling center organizes the space above a cave chamber through nested squares, petals, roundels, borders and directional movement. Individual caves differ by period and program; “the Dunhuang ceiling” is not one fixed design.

Digital Dunhuang's open material library documents examples built around lotuses, cloud forms, lions, dragons, flying figures and the Three Hares motif. These patterns belong to the cave's larger visual and religious environment.

## Why is the lotus often central?

The lotus is important across Buddhist art and may structure the ceiling as a radiating center. Its significance must be read with the surrounding figures, period and cave context rather than assigned one universal color chart.

## Dunhuang Within reflection: returning to the center

The geometry offers a contemporary practice: move through complexity without losing the center. We treat concentric borders as a reminder that attention can return again and again. This is our reflection, clearly separate from a claim about the historical ritual purpose of every ceiling.

## From ceiling pattern to jewelry

Nested geometry can become a pendant, bead cap, clasp or engraving without copying an entire sacred composition. Future designs should name the cave or pattern source when identifiable and avoid presenting decorative fragments as magical seals.
