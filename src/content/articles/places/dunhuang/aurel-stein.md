## The 1907 encounter

Marc Aurel Stein reached the Mogao Caves during his second Central Asian expedition. With the help of his Chinese assistant and interpreter Jiang Xiaowan, he negotiated with Wang Yuanlu for access to the recently opened Library Cave.

## What Stein acquired

Stein purchased many bundles of Chinese and Tibetan manuscripts as well as paintings, textiles, prints and other objects. The 868 [Diamond Sutra](/places/dunhuang/diamond-sutra/) became the most famous individual item, but the importance of the collection lies in its range: religious texts, documents and visual material that illuminate everyday and institutional life.

## Exploration and imperial power

Stein was a highly skilled linguist, surveyor and archaeologist whose publications transformed knowledge of the Silk Roads. He also operated within the resources and assumptions of British imperial scholarship. Wang could not have assessed the archive on equal institutional terms, and the low payment relative to the collection’s importance remains central to criticism.

Acknowledging that context does not require erasing Stein’s scholarship; it requires refusing the older heroic-expedition story as the only frame.

## Where the material went

Objects from Stein’s expeditions are divided among institutions including the British Library, British Museum, Victoria and Albert Museum and National Museum in New Delhi. Different materials—manuscripts, paintings and textiles—entered different custodial systems.

## The role of digitization

The [International Dunhuang Programme](/places/dunhuang/international-dunhuang-programme/) digitally reconnects dispersed collections and makes catalog records and images available across borders. Digital access cannot settle questions of ownership or return, but it reduces the scholarly fragmentation created by dispersal.

Read the story alongside [Wang Yuanlu](/places/dunhuang/wang-yuanlu/) and [Cave 17](/places/dunhuang/cave-17/), not as a standalone biography of a triumphant explorer.
