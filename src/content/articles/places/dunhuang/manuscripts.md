## What are the Dunhuang manuscripts?

The term usually refers to manuscripts, printed documents, paintings, and other objects found in Mogao Cave 17, often called the Library Cave. The cave was sealed around the beginning of the eleventh century and rediscovered in 1900.

The collection is important because it preserves far more than elite religious writing. Buddhist sutras and commentaries appear alongside contracts, inventories, letters, calendars, medical material, travel records, and evidence of daily life.

## What the documents reveal

Religious texts show copying, printing, translation and devotional practice. Contracts and accounts reveal land, loans, labor and taxation. Letters preserve individual voices. Calendars, school exercises, recipes and fragments reused as writing material show how texts circulated beyond formal libraries.

This variety makes Dunhuang essential to social history. A visually plain account can answer questions that a spectacular painting cannot: who paid, what was measured, which languages were understood and how institutions managed everyday obligations.

## A multilingual Silk Road archive

Chinese and Tibetan are prominent, but the wider corpus includes material in Sanskrit, Khotanese, Uyghur, Sogdian, and other languages and scripts. This diversity makes the archive evidence of movement, translation, and cultural contact—not a record of one isolated tradition.

Some manuscripts are bilingual or combine scripts. Others preserve translations whose original versions are lost. Language does not map neatly onto a single ethnicity or religion, so catalog labels must be read with care.

## The Diamond Sutra

One celebrated item from Cave 17 is a Chinese copy of the *Diamond Sutra* dated 11 May 868. The International Dunhuang Programme describes it as the world’s earliest dated printed book.

The fuller description is the earliest known complete dated printed book. The colophon names the donor Wang Jie, while the illustrated frontispiece and joined paper sheets demonstrate a mature woodblock-printing culture. Read the dedicated [Dunhuang Diamond Sutra guide](/places/dunhuang/diamond-sutra/).

## Why the manuscripts are dispersed

In the early twentieth century, large groups of materials left Dunhuang and entered institutions in several countries. That dispersal is part of the collection’s difficult modern history. Today, international digitization helps researchers examine materials that are physically separated.

[Wang Yuanlu](/places/dunhuang/wang-yuanlu/) controlled initial access after reopening the cave. [Aurel Stein](/places/dunhuang/aurel-stein/) and [Paul Pelliot](/places/dunhuang/paul-pelliot/) acquired major groups, while other expeditions and Chinese officials also removed materials. Understanding the archive therefore requires both medieval history and the politics of modern collecting.

## How scholars reconstruct fragments

A scroll can be divided across institutions or catalogued under different expedition numbers. Researchers compare handwriting, paper, joins, titles, colophons and physical dimensions to identify relationships. Digitization makes side-by-side study possible without repeatedly moving fragile originals.

## How to search the archive

Begin with a subject, language, shelfmark or person rather than the generic word “manuscript.” Record the holding institution and catalogue identifier. When using an online image, check rights and attribution rather than assuming that an ancient object is unrestricted in every digital reproduction.
