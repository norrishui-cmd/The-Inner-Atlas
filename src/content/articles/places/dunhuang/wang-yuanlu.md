## A caretaker at an extraordinary site

Wang Yuanlu was a Daoist priest and former soldier who took responsibility for parts of the neglected Mogao cave complex around the end of the nineteenth century. He organized repairs and raised money, though his interventions did not follow modern conservation standards.

## The opening of Cave 17

In 1900, Wang uncovered a sealed chamber off the corridor of Cave 16. The small room contained manuscripts, paintings, textiles and printed material accumulated centuries earlier. It is now known as Cave 17 or the Library Cave.

Calling this a “discovery” does not mean the site lacked a local history. It describes the reopening of a sealed chamber whose contents then entered a new and highly unequal world of officials, collectors and foreign expeditions.

## Transactions with foreign visitors

When Aurel Stein reached Mogao in 1907, Wang allowed access and sold him bundles of material. Paul Pelliot later selected another substantial group. Wang also gave or sold items to Chinese officials and others. Some funds were used for repairs, but the transactions dispersed the archive across institutions.

## Why his legacy is contested

Wang is sometimes presented only as the man who betrayed a national treasure and sometimes romanticized as its humble savior. Neither frame is sufficient. He cared for the site with limited institutional support, made damaging changes, sought official attention and participated in transactions shaped by poverty, power imbalance and the collecting ambitions of the period.

## How to read the story responsibly

Separate what documents establish from later moral simplification. Study Wang alongside the foreign expeditions, Qing and early Republican governance, local conditions and the modern institutions that retain the objects.
