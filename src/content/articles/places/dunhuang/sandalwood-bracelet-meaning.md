## A wood with a long religious history

Sandalwood's use in prayer and ritual is documented across multiple traditions rather than a single one. In Hindu practice, sandalwood paste is applied to deities and worshippers alike as part of puja, and is considered one of the most widely used elements in Hindu and Vedic ceremonial life. In Buddhist practice, sandalwood incense and prayer beads have long been used to support meditation, and the wood is burned in temples across South and East Asia.

## Why sandalwood specifically

Historically, sandalwood's fragrance and durability made it valuable for temple carving, religious statues, and beads that would remain fragrant for a long time. Its use in East Asian Buddhist and Taoist ceremonies, alongside incense materials such as agarwood, is well documented.

## What we won't claim

We will not describe a sandalwood bracelet as having measurable "energy," healing power, or the ability to change fortune. Those are not documented properties—they are claims made by some commercial sellers that go beyond sandalwood's actual religious and cultural history. What is documented is a long tradition of using sandalwood as a material of devotion, focus and ceremony.

## Practical care

Sandalwood is a natural material, not a manufactured one, so it responds to how it's treated. Avoid submerging a sandalwood bracelet in water or exposing it to strong perfumes and lotions, both of which can strip its fragrance and finish over time. Store it away from direct sunlight in a soft pouch when not being worn, and its natural fragrance will typically persist for years.
